# 30-Day Post Fiat Alignment Score Improvement Plan

## Preamble: Diagnosis and Strategy

As of this writing, my Post Fiat alignment profile reflects a system that has achieved meaningful early traction but is entering a critical phase where the gap between infrastructure build-out and live operational consistency becomes the dominant constraint on score improvement. The raw numbers — 15 rewarded tasks, 1 refused task, 0 outstanding tasks, and a recently rewritten context document — tell a partial story. The acceptance rate of approximately 94% (15 accepted out of 16 total) is strong in absolute terms, but a single refusal carries disproportionate weight when the denominator is still small. More importantly, the true risk to alignment at this stage is not the historical refusal record but the forward-looking question of whether engagement remains consistent, task quality holds at a high verification standard, and the context document evolves in lockstep with operational reality.

My diagnosis identifies five dimensions where deliberate, measurable improvement is both possible and necessary. Task acceptance rate is the surface metric that feels most urgent, but it is largely a lagging indicator — the real leverage is in the upstream decision-making that determines which tasks get accepted, how quickly they get completed, and whether the work product clears verification. Completion velocity matters because outstanding tasks signal disengagement, and the current 0 outstanding is a baseline I need to protect, not take for granted. Verification quality is where alignment scores are actually differentiated at the top of the distribution — completing tasks is table stakes; completing them in ways that generate clean verification evidence is what separates good nodes from excellent ones. Context document accuracy is the dimension most directly within my control: every day the document drifts from operational reality is a day the network's model of my capabilities and constraints becomes slightly less useful. And engagement consistency — the simple question of whether I show up daily — is the compounding factor that either amplifies or erodes everything else.

The strategy for the next 30 days is therefore structured around three principles. First, protect the baseline: accept and complete every suitable task without creating new outstanding debt. Second, invest in quality signals: focus not just on task completion but on the specificity and verifiability of work product. Third, maintain document accuracy as a living practice rather than a periodic cleanup exercise. Each week builds on the last, and the four checkpoint milestones at Days 7, 14, 21, and 30 are designed to create accountability against numeric targets rather than subjective impressions of progress.

## Section 2: Corrective Actions by Weak Dimension

**Acceptance Rate Recovery:** Accept and complete the next 10 tasks consecutively without refusal. Before declining any task, apply a two-question test: (1) Is the refusal grounded in a genuine constraint documented in the context doc? (2) Is partial completion possible? Default to partial completion over outright refusal.

**Verification Quality Uplift:** For every task submitted, include at minimum: a link or artifact demonstrating the output, a one-sentence description of the verification method, and explicit mapping of the deliverable to the task prompt. Do not submit bare completions.

**Context Document Cadence:** Review and update the context document every Sunday. The update should answer: what changed this week, what was completed, and whether any constraints or capabilities need revision. Log the update date in the document itself.

**Engagement Consistency:** Establish a daily check-in window (same time each day). Even on days with no active tasks, log into the network and confirm current status. Treat missed check-ins as a leading indicator requiring correction within 24 hours.

## Commitment Statement

This plan functions as both a diagnostic instrument and a public commitment device. The targets are calibrated to be achievable without being trivial — they require consistent daily engagement, deliberate attention to verification quality, and disciplined context document maintenance. The single refusal in the historical record is not a crisis, but it is a signal worth taking seriously when the total task count is still modest. Every task completed with clean verification evidence over the next 30 days shifts the statistical weight of that refusal toward negligibility.

The deeper purpose of this plan is not to game a score but to build the operational habits that produce high alignment scores as a byproduct: showing up every day, doing good work, being honest in the context document about what is actually true, and defaulting to completion rather than refusal when the path is uncertain.
