Neighbourhood Selection Rationale

Three Osaka neighbourhoods were selected to represent meaningfully distinct regulatory profiles, tourist demand intensities, and kanrisha supply conditions — not simply the three highest-ADR markets. The analytical value of this model depends on the neighbourhoods being genuinely differentiated: a comparison of three similar high-density tourist zones would produce undifferentiated conclusions. The selection logic below makes the differentiation explicit.

Namba/Dotonbori (Naniwa Ward / Chuo Ward boundary) is the highest-density tourist zone and the regulatory epicentre of the Osaka STR crackdown. It holds Special Zone status permitting year-round operation but faces the steepest enforcement trajectory: 17 special zone properties packed into a 500m² area in Naniwa Ward drove the complaint surge that precipitated city-wide suspension. New applications suspended as of October 2025 with formal effective date May 30, 2026. Existing licensed properties now operate as a constrained-supply market. Kanrisha availability is highest here — multiple licensed management companies cluster specifically around Dotonbori due to density economics, which keeps management fees at the market floor of 15% rather than the 17–20% charged in lower-density zones.

Tennoji (Tennoji Ward) was selected as the mid-tier market: a transit hub with high tourist density and an active hotel conversion pipeline, but lower complaint rates than Naniwa and a meaningfully different guest profile. Tennoji's proximity to Shin-Osaka and the Osaka loop line creates demand from business travellers and domestic tourists in addition to the international leisure segment that dominates Dotonbori. The grandfathered licence structure here is the same as Naniwa — new apps suspended, 365-day operation rights for existing permits — but enforcement intensity is lower and the kanrisha market is less concentrated, pushing management fees toward 17.5–18% of gross.

Fukushima/Nakatsu (Fukushima Ward) is the emerging market selection: a residential-commercial mixed ward on the Osaka loop line between Umeda and the airport rail corridor, with a developing F&B scene and lower tourist saturation than the other two. Critically, Fukushima operates under standard Minpaku Law (180-day cap) rather than Special Zone rules — meaning new registrations remain available, unlike Naniwa and Tennoji. This is the only neighbourhood in this model where a new operator can actually enter without acquiring an existing licensed property. The trade-off is the 180-day ceiling on operating nights. ADR runs lower (¥11,000–13,000 for a 1BR versus ¥14,000+ in Namba) but acquisition costs are materially below the tourist core. Kanrisha availability is lower per unit area, pushing management fees to 18–20% — the wide end of the market range.

180-day cap dominates the model: The single biggest structural disadvantage in Fukushima is not ADR or management fees — it is the available night ceiling. The model only works at 180-day utilisation rates approaching 75%+ — a threshold that requires near-perfect marketing and pricing execution.

§6

Go / No-Go Thresholds

The following thresholds are derived from the sensitivity matrix and P&L models above. They represent the minimum conditions under which an individual non-resident operator (without institutional scale to internalise the kanrisha function) can expect positive NOI — not positive cash flow after financing, which depends on acquisition price and leverage structure. Operators should apply both the occupancy threshold and the management fee ceiling simultaneously; either condition alone is insufficient.

The Fukushima no-go threshold reveals why the 180-day cap is more structurally damaging than management fee variation: even at the market-floor management fee of 15%, the model cannot achieve positive levered cash flow at occupancy rates that are operationally realistic for a new operator in a non-tourist-core ward. The go/no-go inflection is not a marginal decision — it is a structural ceiling imposed by the operating day constraint.

§7

Ranked Recommendation & Thesis Invalidation

Rank 1 · Highest Conviction · GO
Namba / Dotonbori — Naniwa Ward / Chuo Ward
The strongest site on every dimension that matters for individual operator returns: highest ADR of the three markets, lowest management fee (driven by kanrisha density economics), lowest minimum occupancy threshold for positive NOI (37%), and the widest margin of safety in the sensitivity matrix (positive NOI in all 6 cells). The suspension of new Special Zone applications is a near-term entry barrier but a medium-term structural tailwind — existing licensed properties become scarcer and therefore more valuable on both a revenue and resale basis. The single hard condition: only acquire a property that already holds a valid Special Zone licence. A new acquisition that defaults to 180-day standard minpaku registration cuts annual gross revenue by approximately 50% and inverts the entire investment case.
Rank 2 · Conditional
Tennoji — Tennoji Ward
Viable for an operator who can secure an existing Special Zone licence at below-market acquisition cost — specifically ¥22M or below for a 1BR, which brings the financing burden low enough that 60–65% occupancy produces positive levered cash flow. At the illustrative ¥26M midpoint, the model requires 62% occupancy for positive cash flow after debt service, which is achievable but leaves no margin of safety against the ADR compression scenario. The hotel conversion pipeline near Abeno Harukas is the primary ongoing competitive headwind: as formal hotel supply increases in the ward, STR ADR faces structural downward pressure that the Tennoji model has less capacity to absorb than Namba.
Rank 3 · No-Go
Fukushima / Nakatsu — Fukushima Ward (Standard Minpaku)
The 180-day operating ceiling is a structural disqualifier at current acquisition costs and management fee levels. Positive levered cash flow requires 86% occupancy of permitted nights — an unrealistic operational target for a new entrant in a non-tourist-core ward. The lower acquisition cost advantage is fully offset by the revenue ceiling. The only scenario in which Fukushima becomes viable is if an operator can: (a) acquire at ¥12M or below (a distressed-only scenario), or (b) access a Special Zone licence that permits year-round operation — but new Special Zone applications are also suspended here, making (b) unavailable to new entrants. The model works as a long-term capital appreciation play at compressed acquisition cost, not as a current-income STR investment.

Thesis Invalidation Conditions — Namba/Dotonbori (Highest Conviction)

Thesis invalidation — if any of these occur, reassess immediately
01
MLIT enacts formal revision of Article 11(1)(iii) permitting remote kanrisha certification via technology. This eliminates the captive vendor structure and triggers management fee compression toward 8–12% — structurally positive for operators but requires re-underwriting all existing management agreements and likely triggers market-entry by new competitors. The thesis changes from supply-constrained scarcity play to competitive operational efficiency play.
02
Osaka Prefecture's Cabinet Office proposal (filed November 2025) receives national approval and Governor Yoshimura's stated intent to fully terminate the Special Zone Tokku Minpaku programme is enacted. Existing licensed properties would revert to 180-day standard minpaku operation, cutting Namba/Dotonbori annual gross revenue by ~50% and inverting the investment case from GO to NO-GO. Monitor the Cabinet Office review calendar and any Diet legislative action on the Special Zone framework through May 2026 effective date.
03
Naniwa Ward complaint rate accelerates above 400 complaints per quarter (versus the ~400 per year that drove the 2025 suspension), triggering ward-level licence revocation orders against existing Special Zone operators. This is the enforcement escalation risk specific to Naniwa Ward's density profile — 17 properties in 500m² creates a concentrated complaint surface. If revocation orders begin, the existing-licence scarcity premium inverts to an existing-licence liability discount.
04
ADR compression exceeds 15% sustained for two consecutive quarters — the scenario in which all 6 cells of the Namba sensitivity matrix produce negative levered cash flow. This would require either a significant supply shock (e.g., major hotel openings in Namba/Chuo ward absorbing inbound demand) or a demand collapse (macro Japan tourism downturn, yen appreciation above ¥120/USD materially reducing inbound visitor economics).

Synthesis

The Osaka STR market in Q1 2026 is a constrained-supply, enforcement-elevated environment where the investable opportunity is narrow but well-defined: existing Special Zone licensed properties in Namba/Dotonbori, acquired below ¥35M for a 1BR, with verified licence status and a 16% or below management agreement in place. The regulatory cost floors identified across this analysis — the 10-minute proximity mandate driving kanrisha fee stickiness, the suspended application pipeline driving supply constraint, the ward-level complaint trajectory driving enforcement risk — are not transient conditions. They are structural features of the current regulatory settlement that will persist until the Cabinet Office makes a definitive ruling on the Special Zone programme, likely before Q3 2026. The window between now and that ruling is the period of maximum uncertainty — and for existing-licence holders, maximum scarcity premium.

An investor who cannot access an existing Special Zone licence at the acquisition cost thresholds identified above should not attempt to enter the Osaka STR market via standard minpaku registration. The 180-day ceiling makes the standard route economically unviable at current property prices and management fee levels in all three neighbourhoods modelled.
