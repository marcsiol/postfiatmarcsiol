§01

Universe Selection & Screening Rationale

Five TSE-listed J-REITs were selected with meaningful Osaka hospitality or residential exposure. The selection criterion was direct relevance to the minpaku regulatory disruption: each REIT competes with, hosts, or is exposed to the cost dynamics of short-term rental operators in Osaka's tourist core. The regulatory cost floors identified in prior work — the 10-minute proximity mandate driving kanrisha fee stickiness at 15–20% of gross, the Special Zone application suspension, and ward-level enforcement escalation — create differential competitive headwinds across this universe that are not yet priced into all names.

The central analytical question is not which REITs are directly exposed to minpaku regulation (none own minpaku assets directly) but which REITs compete in Osaka's accommodation market against a pool of STR operators facing structurally rising compliance costs — and whether that competitive dynamic is beneficial (pricing power for formal hotels) or harmful (supply-constrained STR compressing hotel demand in ways the market hasn't modelled). The secondary question is which REITs hold direct-operation Osaka assets with management cost structures that parallel the STR compliance floor.

Scoring Matrix — 4 Dimensions

Margin vulnerability estimates sensitivity to the regulatory cost floor model from prior work — specifically how the 30–33% compliance floor (platform + kanrisha fees) on STR operators affects formal hotel pricing power. Valuation scores current P/NAV premium/discount relative to the sector median. Catalyst proximity scores the nearness of a material regulatory, operational, or financial event within 12 months. Higher scores indicate higher risk/opportunity depending on direction. Scores of 4–5 on Osaka concentration + margin vulnerability represent the highest regulatory alpha opportunity.

§03

Margin Compression Estimates — Regulatory Cost Floor Impact

For the three REITs with meaningful Osaka exposure and direct-operation or lodging-focused business models (INV, Ichigo, NHR), the regulatory cost floor model from the Osaka STR Feasibility Memo is applied to estimate the margin compression pathway. The mechanism is competitive rather than direct: the STR sector's mandated compliance floor of 30–33% of gross revenue (platform fee + kanrisha fee) reduces the number of properties that can profitably undercut formal hotel pricing at mid-market ADR levels. As enforcement tightens and the Special Zone suspension removes new supply, STR-driven ADR pressure in the ¥10,000–15,000/night range diminishes — which should support formal hotel RevPAR. The downside scenario is enforcement slack: if complaint-driven revocations stall and STR supply remains, the competitive pressure on mid-market hotel ADR persists.

Key insight: The regulatory cost floor creates an asymmetric competitive dynamic. For formal hotels in the ¥12,000–18,000/night ADR range (where INV's Shinsaibashi assets and NHR's Namba anchor operate), the STR compliance floor at ¥11,900–14,000 effective ADR minimum creates a price support beneath which compliant STR operators cannot profitably go. STR operators who cannot reach this floor will exit. This is structurally pro-RevPAR for mid-market Osaka hotels — a positive for INV's Osaka assets and potentially transformative for NHR's Namba concentration.

NHR is the highest-conviction name in the universe. Its APA Hotel Namba Minami Daikokucho Ekimae sits directly in Naniwa Ward — the same ward identified as the epicentre of Osaka's minpaku complaint surge and the geographic locus of the enforcement crackdown. With only 23 total properties, a single Namba asset represents approximately 18–22% of portfolio NAV by acquisition cost. The DPU forecast step-down from ¥2,455 (Nov FY2025) to ¥2,008 (May FY2026) reflects operator guidance that already prices in some Osaka demand moderation post-Expo. The regulatory thesis is that this step-down is conservative — the Special Zone suspension, combined with the Nuisance Minpaku Eradication Team's operational posture, is removing mid-market STR competition in Naniwa Ward faster than hotel consensus models assume.

The 8.5% forward yield is the highest in the universe and reflects the small-cap risk premium, the DPU step-down, and the market's scepticism about Osaka demand sustainability. The regulatory alpha here is that the market is pricing the Expo tail-off as a demand story when the supply story (constrained STR competition) is actually moving in NHR's favour simultaneously.

Synthesis & Ranked Watchlist

The regulatory alpha in the Osaka STR market flows to formal hotel operators in two ways simultaneously: the compliance cost floor prevents STR operators from competing on price below approximately ¥10,200–11,900/night, and the Special Zone suspension combined with enforcement escalation is physically removing supply from Naniwa Ward — the most tourist-dense micro-market in Osaka. Both effects are pro-RevPAR for the Namba-area hotel assets held by INV and NHR. The market has not priced either effect into either name at current yield levels.

The ranked watchlist for action is: (1) NHR (3472) at or below current yield levels as the highest-conviction play on Namba supply contraction — enter if unit price touches ¥95,000 or below, confirming a yield above 8.5% on FY2026 DPU guidance; (2) INV (8963) as a more liquid expression of the same thesis with partial variable-rent insulation — enter on Hotel MyStays Shinsaibashi Dec 2025 FP confirmation of RevPAR ≥ ¥18,000; (3) JHR (8985) as a quality hold — no new position unless yield weakness opens above 5.5%. Ichigo (3463) and Hoshino (3287) are not actionable on this thesis — Ichigo lacks the catalyst clarity for the thesis to crystallise, and Hoshino's luxury positioning means it neither benefits from nor is harmed by the mid-market STR competitive dynamics this analysis tracks.

The May 30, 2026 effective date of the Osaka Special Zone suspension is the single most important near-term catalyst date for this entire watchlist. It formalises what has been building since October 2025 and creates the documented supply constraint that formal hotel RevPAR models will eventually have to reflect. Position sizing ahead of that date, with NHR as the primary vehicle, is the deployment strategy this screening model supports.
