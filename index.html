Section I   National-Level Framework

The Private Lodging Business Act and Special Zone Architecture

Japan's short-term rental market operates under two overlapping legal frameworks. The Private Lodging Business Act (住宅宿泊事業法, Minpaku-ho, Law No. 65 of 2017, effective June 2018) established the primary national framework: operators may rent residential properties for up to 180 nights per year without a hotel license, subject to municipal registration, a licensed kanrisha (management company) for absentee owners, and Article 11's 10-minute physical proximity requirement. Separately, the National Strategic Special Zone Act (国家戦略特別区域法) created a parallel pathway — Special Zone Minpaku (特区民泊) — permitting year-round operation in designated municipalities, subject to a minimum stay of two consecutive nights and a 25㎡ minimum room size. Osaka City was among the first municipalities designated under this special zone framework in 2014, predating the 2018 national law.

The national law explicitly delegates supplementary rule-making authority to local governments through Article 18, allowing municipalities to impose operating day restrictions, area bans, and enhanced conditions beyond the national floor. This delegation is the legal basis for all ward-level and city-level tightening documented in this dossier.

Section II   Osaka Ward-Level Variations

Geographic Regulatory Map: Three Named Wards

Osaka City's regulatory environment differs from the national baseline across several dimensions. Under the Special Zone designation, Osaka had historically permitted year-round operation — making it structurally more permissive than Tokyo (where most wards impose weekday bans or seasonal caps) and Kyoto (where residential zone minpaku is restricted to a roughly two-month winter window, January 16 to March 15). However, the ward-level picture within Osaka is not uniform, and the September–November 2025 regulatory pivot has altered the operative framework significantly.

Section III   Enforcement Actions & Compliance Data

Complaint Trajectory, Enforcement Architecture, and Penalty Structure

2019
First Ordinance Amendment: Osaka City amended its special zone minpaku ordinance, establishing resident consultation desks, strengthening on-site inspection authority, and increasing penalties for illegal operations. Described as insufficient by city officials — did not provide a "fundamental solution."
FY2023 (reported mid-2024)
Complaint Surge: Osaka City received 399 official complaints about minpaku in FY2023 — more than double the prior year. Issues: late-night noise, garbage disposal, fire safety, one-night stay violations. By end of August 2025, complaints in the current fiscal year had already reached 250, tracking for another doubling.
July 2025
Task Force Established: Osaka City convened an internal task force to study countermeasures for the special zone minpaku complaint escalation. Marked formal recognition that the 2019 ordinance amendment was structurally insufficient.
September 30, 2025
Suspension Announced: Mayor Yokoyama announced suspension of new special zone minpaku applications. City stated intent to: (1) establish a dedicated enforcement team, (2) investigate all existing minpaku within city limits, (3) prepare license revocation manuals for violating operators, (4) publish new cancellation guidelines by November 2025. City confirmed 27 of 34 Osaka Prefecture municipalities had expressed intent to opt out of the special zones system — up from 7 on September 1, a rapid acceleration over 29 days.
November 18, 2025
National Proposal Filed: Osaka Prefectural Government and participating municipalities formally submitted the suspension proposal at the Cabinet Office National Strategic Special Zones area meeting, with a formal effective date of May 30, 2026 for cessation of new applications.
May 30, 2026 (effective)
Formal Suspension Takes Effect: New special zone minpaku applications cease across Osaka City and 28 other municipalities in Osaka Prefecture. Existing licensed properties grandfathered — but designated primary targets of "Nuisance Minpaku Eradication Team." Unlicensed operations: fines up to ¥1 million. License revocation threshold lowered for operators with repeated disturbance complaints.

Section IV   Pending & Proposed Changes

Regulatory Pipeline: Timeline Estimates and Open Questions

Three regulatory developments are pending or in process as of Q1 2026, each with material implications for operator margin and J-REIT evaluation.

1. National Special Zone Reform — Local Authority Enhancement

Osaka City has formally requested that the central government revise the National Strategic Special Zone framework to grant local governments expanded authority to impose their own stricter regulations and conduct guidance and supervision of special zone minpaku. This is a request for a structural change to the national framework — not merely an Osaka-specific ordinance. If granted, it would enable other municipalities currently within special zones to apply similar tightening without requiring a formal Cabinet Office-level process for each action. Timeline: subject to national legislative calendar; no confirmed Diet consideration date as of March 2026.

2. Nuisance Minpaku Eradication Team — Enforcement Ramp

Osaka City committed to establishing a dedicated enforcement team and publishing license revocation guidelines by the end of November 2025. These guidelines were in preparation as of the October reporting. The team represents a permanent structural addition to Osaka's compliance enforcement architecture — distinct from the periodic inspections that characterised pre-2025 enforcement. Operationally, this means grandfathered special zone operators face ongoing, not episodic, compliance scrutiny. Timeline: team in operation from Q4 2025; enforcement intensity expected to increase through 2026.

3. Governor Yoshimura's Program Termination Proposal

Governor Yoshimura announced his intention to completely end the Special Zone Tokku Minpaku program, not merely suspend new applications. His stated justification: hotel supply has caught up with demand, removing the original policy rationale, and community backlash has reached unsustainable levels. This is politically more aggressive than the city's "temporary pause" framing and, if enacted, would represent a permanent closure of the year-round operation pathway for new operators. The formal legal mechanism for terminating a National Strategic Special Zone designation requires national-level action. Timeline: uncertain; dependent on Cabinet Office engagement and national policy appetite. Risk level for existing operators: elevated.

Synthesis   Underpriced Regulatory Vectors

Three Regulatory Vectors Most Underpriced by Market Participants

The following synthesis identifies the highest-impact regulatory developments that are, based on the secondary evidence compiled above, most likely to be inadequately priced into current operator valuations and J-REIT analysis. "Underpriced" means that standard top-down market analysis — which typically captures national-level rule changes but misses ground-level enforcement acceleration and municipal opt-out dynamics — is likely producing optimistic margin assumptions for affected operators.

#1HIGHEST
The Municipal Opt-Out Acceleration Rate
Vector Type: Geographic Scope Expansion  ·  Visibility: Low
The acceleration from 7 to 27 Osaka Prefecture municipalities opting out of the special zones system in a single 29-day period (September 1 to September 30, 2025) is the most underpriced data point in the publicly available evidence. Standard investment analysis typically models Osaka as a single regulatory unit. The reality is that the tightening is a cascading municipal-level phenomenon — each opt-out reduces the geographic footprint available for special zone year-round operation, compressing the revenue base for operators whose portfolio density assumptions were built on broader city access.
Why Underpriced The opt-out data was reported in Japanese-language press and secondary aggregators, not in English-language REIT disclosures or sell-side research. Any J-REIT evaluation relying on a simple "Osaka exposure" flag without mapping portfolio units against the 29-municipality suspension will overstate operative revenue capacity.
#2HIGH
Grandfathered Licence Premium vs. Enforcement Drag
Vector Type: Existing Asset Revaluation  ·  Visibility: Medium
The suspension of new special zone applications has created an apparent scarcity premium for existing licensed properties — they are the only assets that can legally operate year-round in Osaka under the special zone framework. This premium is real and has been noted in investor commentary. What is underpriced is the countervailing enforcement drag: grandfathered properties are now the primary targets of Osaka's new "Nuisance Minpaku Eradication Team," operating under faster license revocation guidelines and mandatory platform reporting of violations. The net effect on existing asset values depends on how aggressively the team enforces, but the standard "scarcity premium" framing captures the supply restriction without discounting for the increased compliance tail risk on the demand side of the same assets.
Why Underpriced Investor commentary has uniformly noted the scarcity premium (licenses become more valuable when new ones cannot be issued). The offsetting enforcement intensification receives little coverage because it has not yet produced visible license revocations at scale — but the revocation guidelines and enforcement team mandate are both confirmed in the public record.
#3MEDIUM
National Framework Reform Request — Downstream Implications
Vector Type: Structural / National Precedent  ·  Visibility: Low
Osaka City's formal request to the central government for expanded local authority over special zone minpaku regulation has received almost no coverage in English-language investment analysis. If granted, it would establish a template that other municipalities within National Strategic Special Zones could use to impose similar tightening without requiring a Cabinet Office-level process per city. The STR sector's playbook of absorbing municipal tightening city-by-city would face a structural shift: a single national rule change enabling simultaneous local tightening across all special zone municipalities would compress the addressable market for year-round operation in ways that cannot be hedged by geographic diversification across special zone cities.
Why Underpriced The request is in process but has not produced a legislative proposal. Investors rationally discount unconfirmed regulatory risk. However, the combination of Osaka's demonstrated willingness to act, the national government's 60-million-visitor-by-2030 tourism target (which creates some political resistance to over-tightening), and the pending framework review creates a binary outcome risk that is not reflected in current operator valuations.
