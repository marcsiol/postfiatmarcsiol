§1Neighbourhood Selection Criteria

Three neighbourhoods were selected to represent meaningfully distinct regulatory environments, tourist profiles, and Airbnb market maturities — rather than simply picking the three highest-ADR markets. The selection criteria weighed four factors equally: demonstrated inbound tourist volume, legal operating environment for minpaku, proximity to transit and primary attractions, and differentiation in the Airbnb-versus-hotel value proposition.

Shinjuku (Tokyo) was selected as the high-volume, high-regulation baseline. With over 600,000 guest nights recorded in Tokyo in a two-month window in late 2024, the demand case is unambiguous. The regulatory case is more complex: Shinjuku ward restricts residential-zone Airbnb to weekends only (Friday noon to Monday noon), creating an effective annual ceiling of approximately 150 operating days. This makes Shinjuku the most instructive test of whether the regulatory cap kills the financial case.

Dotonbori / Namba (Osaka) was selected as the regulatory counterpoint. Osaka City operates under the National Strategic Special Zone framework, meaning qualifying properties can operate year-round without the 180-day national cap, provided guests stay a minimum of two consecutive nights. Osaka drew the highest international guest concentration of the three cities, with 88.8% of Airbnb guests being non-Japanese as of 2024–2025. With new Special Zone applications suspended from late 2025, existing licensed properties become a constrained supply with rising scarcity value.

Higashiyama (Kyoto) was selected as the high-ADR, low-volume, high-friction market. Kyoto's historic districts apply the most restrictive overlay rules of any major Japanese tourist city: residential-zone minpaku is restricted to January 15–March 15 only, an effective cap of just 59 operating days per year in much of the area. Hotels and ryokans dominate the accommodation market here. The Airbnb value proposition in Higashiyama is therefore based almost entirely on the space-per-dollar premium over comparable traditional lodging — not on revenue volume.

HOA risk: Many Shinjuku condominiums have bylaws prohibiting minpaku — verify before purchase.
Conditional
Regulatory ceiling limits revenue potential. The demand fundamentals in Shinjuku are excellent — 57% of Tokyo Airbnb guests are international, ADRs are among the highest in Japan, and occupancy on permitted days is strong. However, the weekend-only restriction in residential zones caps annual gross revenue at roughly ¥1.87M for a standard 1BR, which is insufficient to justify purchase prices that commonly reach ¥60–90M for a Shinjuku apartment. The math works only if the property is in a commercial zone (full operating rights), already hotel-licensed, or acquired at a yield-justified price well below market average.

Annual gross for this hypothetical 1BR sits at approximately ¥3.28M (~$21,160). The Airbtics city-wide Osaka average for 2024–2025 was ¥3M ($24K), aligning well with this model. The key variable is Special Zone licensing status: without it, a new operator falls under the 180-day national cap, cutting annual revenue to approximately ¥1.64M — a 50% haircut.

Regulatory Environment

Special Zone status: Osaka City designated under National Strategic Special Zone — year-round operation possible but requires minimum 2-night stays and a separate permit application. New applications suspended: Osaka City announced suspension of new Special Zone minpaku applications circa October 2025, citing ~400 noise/garbage complaints in FY2024. Existing licensed properties face no operational change — supply is now artificially constrained. Fallback: New acquisitions default to 180-day Minpaku Law registration. Accommodation tax: ¥100–¥300 per guest per night. Management fee benchmark: 15–20% of gross for third-party managers.
Go
Strongest revenue-per-permitted-day of the three markets. Year-round operation rights, the highest international guest concentration (88.8%), and the most favourable acquisition-cost-to-revenue ratio in the three-market comparison. The suspension of new Special Zone applications is a near-term headwind for new entrants but a medium-term tailwind for existing licensed properties, which become scarcer and therefore more valuable on resale. The go verdict carries one firm condition: target only properties that already hold a valid Special Zone licence or a Hotel Business Act licence — new applications face an uncertain timeline. Minpaku-law-only properties in Osaka should be modelled at 180-day revenue, not 365-day.

The residential-zone operating restriction reduces Higashiyama Airbnb investment to an income supplement rather than a standalone revenue asset. Monthly gross during the Jan–Mar window runs approximately ¥390,000 (~$2,516) — attractive on a per-night basis but confined to a 59-day annual window. The machiya segment is the exception: a well-restored entire-house listing can earn ¥2.3–3.1M in the permitted window at premium ADRs, but acquisition and restoration costs for authentic machiya routinely exceed ¥50–80M.

Regulatory Environment

Residential districts: Minpaku permitted January 15–March 15 only (~59 days). Off-season letting via long-term or medium-term rental requires separate contracts. Commercial and hotel zones: Year-round operation possible under Hotel Business Act licence (kan-i shukusho 簡易宿所) — this is the correct path for machiya investment. Minimum stay: Kyoto initially required 2-night minimums; now eased in many zones but verify by specific parcel. Registration requirement: 100% of Kyoto Airbnb listings as of July 2025 carry a short-term rental licence number. Accommodation tax: ¥200–¥1,000 per guest per night — the highest in Japan, scaling with room rate. Management: Non-resident owners require 10-minute-proximity caretaker.
No-Go
Residential-zone operating restriction makes standard Airbnb investment unviable. At 59 permitted days per year and a 1BR ADR of ¥21,000, gross annual revenue is under ¥1M — insufficient to service acquisition debt on any central Higashiyama property. The space premium over hotels is real and meaningful, and demand is strong when permitted, but neither compensates for the structural operating constraint. The exception is a Hotel Business Act licensed machiya acquired below ¥60M in a commercial zone — this format generates viable year-round income and commands strong guest premiums. Residential-zone Airbnb in Kyoto should be modelled exclusively as a secondary income stream on a property acquired for other primary reasons, not as the investment thesis itself.

§7Go / No-Go Rankings

The three neighbourhoods are ranked by investment attractiveness, defined as the combination of regulatory operating freedom, revenue potential per permitted operating day, space-premium value proposition to guests, and acquisition cost relative to projected income. Verdicts assume a non-resident individual investor without existing Japanese hotel licences.

Rank Neighbourhood Verdict Est. Annual Gross (1BR) Key Constraint
1 Dotonbori / Namba, Osaka GO ¥3.28M (~$21,160) Must acquire existing licensed property (new permits suspended)
2 Shinjuku, Tokyo CONDITIONAL ¥1.87M (~$12,065) Weekend-only restriction in residential zones; commercial zone or hotel licence required for full revenue
3 Higashiyama, Kyoto NO-GO ¥994K (~$6,410) 59-day residential operating window; investment case requires Hotel Business Act licence in commercial zone

Ranking Rationale

Dotonbori / Namba is the clear first-ranked market on every dimension except current entry difficulty. Year-round operating rights, the widest space premium of the three markets (98% more floor area per yen versus business hotels), the highest international guest share (88.8%), and annual gross revenue that is 75% above Shinjuku's. The suspension of new Special Zone permits as of late 2025 is a structural constraint for new buyers, but it simultaneously increases the scarcity value of existing licensed inventory. An investor who identifies and acquires a properly licensed property in the Namba / Dotonbori corridor is buying into constrained supply with rising inbound demand — the Osaka Expo effect continues to drive forward tourism projections. The conditional risk is operational: at 15–20% management fees, the net yield on a standard 1BR at ¥30–45M acquisition cost is tight, and the 2-night minimum stay requirement under Special Zone rules can reduce booking conversion rates.

Shinjuku is conditionally viable but only for a specific property type. The demand fundamentals are Japan's strongest — over half of all Japan guest nights flow through Tokyo, and Shinjuku's ADR leads all residential wards. The problem is purely regulatory: a residential-zone property is limited to approximately ¥1.87M annual gross, which generates a sub-2% gross yield on a ¥100M purchase price. The conditional go applies only to: (a) commercial-zone properties with full operating rights, (b) existing hotel-licensed units, or (c) properties acquired well below ¥60M. At current central Shinjuku acquisition prices, the Airbnb model requires hotel-licence-level operating freedom to pencil out. Without it, long-term letting or corporate housing produces a more stable yield profile.

Higashiyama is a no-go for standard Airbnb investment. The 59-day residential-zone operating cap reduces annual gross to under ¥1M for a 1BR — a figure that cannot service acquisition debt or justify the complexity of licensing and management in Japan's most regulation-intensive tourist city. The space premium over ryokans is the highest of the three markets (120%), and demand during the permitted winter window is strong, but neither metric rescues the revenue arithmetic. The correct investment structure in Higashiyama for income-generating short-stay accommodation is a Hotel Business Act licensed machiya in a commercial or mixed-use zone, which requires specialist acquisition expertise, renovation budgets typically exceeding ¥20–30M on top of purchase price, and operator-level compliance knowledge. This is not a retail Airbnb investment — it is a hospitality business acquisition.
